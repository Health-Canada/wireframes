Why was the Drug and Health Product Register created?

In carrying out its mandate, Health Canada recognizes the importance of making information on health products available to Canadians. This information has been publically available, but it was previously stored in various databases.

Each database has different search tools and capabilities. Consumers, patients, and industry and health professionals have said that information is often hard to find. They find it difficult to search and sometimes difficult to understand.

The DHPR was developed to address these concerns. It supports the federal government's policy on Open Government. It also supports Health Canada's commitment to greater transparency and openness.

Top of page

What are its main features and benefits?

Main Features

  • The DHPR combines health product information from different locations within Health Canada into a single portal. The portal is easy to find and use.
  • More and new types of information can be added as it becomes available. This information is scalable in content and size.
  • The DHPR was designed to include more health products (i.e. natural health products), new functions and more in-depth consumer information.
  • It provides a user-friendly side effect reporting capability for consumers and health professionals.

Benefits

  • The DHPR is mobile device and user friendly.
  • It is a single search platform.
  • Canadians will easily be able to learn more about the health products they are using or planning to use. They can also report suspected side effects they may encounter.
  • Reported side effect information will be easily accessible and in a central location.

Top of page

Who will benefit from using the Drug and Health Product Register?

The public will benefit the most from using the DHPR. However, other groups may benefit as well as new information is added. New information will include drug decisions, drug product information and data on other types of health products.

Top of page

What type of information is found in the Drug and Health Product Register?

The DHPR contains basic information for selected marketed drugs and biological products. Additional details, such as side effects and drug details are available for a limited number of products. Over time, these additional details will be available for all health products marketed in Canada.

For complete listings of side effects reported to Health Canada, visit the Canada Vigilance Adverse Reaction Online Database.

Top of page

Can I search information in the Drug and Health Product Register?

Yes. You can use the DHPR to search for information about drugs. You can search by:

  • brand name,
  • active ingredient, or
  • drug identification number (DIN).

You can also use a wild character (*) in your search. A wildcard is a character that may be substituted for any of a defined subset of all possible characters

What are the technical requirements

You can navigate the DHPR using any mobile device (such as a tablet or a smart phone). The information displayed will be adapted for your device.

To properly view and use the DHPR, your internet browser will need to support HTML5. It's recommended that your computer have the latest version of one of the more widely used internet browsers.

Top of page

Where does the Drug and Health Product Register source data come from?

The DHPR collects information from multiple sources and presents it in a more user-friendly way. Information comes from existing Health Canada data sources. Data sources include the Drug Product Database and the Canada Vigilance Adverse Reaction Online Database.

For a while, the same information will be on the DHPR and other data sources on the Health Canada website. This is necessary until all databases are fully combined with the DHPR. While the DHPR is being developed, there may be differences between data sources. In these cases, the existing public data source is the accurate information warehouse.

Top of page

What is the difference between Adverse Reaction and Side Effect?

Both adverse reaction and side effect refer to the same thing. The term side effect is more commonly used in everyday language. The term adverse reaction is used by health professionals, such as doctors and pharmacists.

Top of page

Why are there inconsistencies in reports on side effects?

Throughout the lifecycle of a product, companies can go through buy-outs, mergers and/or take on licensing agreements. When this happens, the brand name of a drug may change.

When you search for a drug product name in the DHPR, only the most current product name will come up. Because of this, the search will only show reported side effects for the most current product name marketed in Canada.

If you want to see the complete list of all side effects, please refer to the data sources. If any discrepancies are found inside the DHPR, the data sources should always be considered the valid source of information.

Top of page

Why did the form to voluntary report side effect change?

Minor administrative changes help users to voluntarily report suspected side effects. Consumers, patients and health professionals can report side effects using this new feature. They can also continue to report using the MedEffect portal.

Top of page

Why are the drug details (also known as Part III: Consumer Information) not always available in the Drug and Health Product Register?

The first phase of the DHPR will only include information about marketed pharmaceutical and biological human drug products.

For the initial release scheduled for winter 2015, only a subset of drug products (approximately 350 drugs) will have drug details. The selection standards was based on top prescribed medication in Canada in 2013.

The DHPR will build on its content until all PM Part III and associated side effects can be found using the DHPR.

Drug details may not be available for a drug product. If this is the case then users can try searching the active ingredient(s) to find other similar products. However, product monographs containing the same active ingredient(s) may differ in their:

  • formulations,
  • dosage forms,
  • indications, and
  • contraindications.

Users are advised to consult the specific product monograph to ensure accurate information.

Top of page

How does the Drug and Health Product Register align with what is being done internationally?

Similar initiatives have been done in the USA (U.S. Food Drug and Administration) and Australia (Therapeutic Goods Administration). An initiative has also taken place in the European Union.
